Connie Donohue says trees are taking over her block. She’s spent four decades in the same home, located in a quiet south Brooklyn neighborhood called Marine Park. For about as long, she recalls roots unearthing sidewalk tiles and creeping into sewers; branches sprawling downward and blocking street signs.

“It’s a problem. A lot of the trees are overgrown,” Donohue said. “There’s a general very bad feeling about the tree maintenance in the neighborhood.”

Donohue isn’t alone. Overgrown trees are a chronic concern in Marine Park, a new data investigation reveals. But many residents worry their calls for assistance have gone unheard by the New York City Department of Parks & Recreation, which is charged with maintaining trees on public land.

The Marine Park zip code ranks first in calls to the city’s 311 service line over tree issues. The area also ranks third citywide in calls per tree, so it’s not just that Marine Park has more tree cover than most neighborhoods, either.

At a first glance, overgrown trees might not seem like an issue. But branches can block streets. walkways and power lines, and roots can interfere with sidewalks and plumbing. Dead trees and branches can pose particular risk, as they can unexpectedly fall on people or property — the consequences of which can be dangerous and even deadly.

In Marine Park, residents call overgrown trees a daily issue.

“We receive complaints daily about overgrown trees with hanging branches,” said Theresa Scavo, chairperson of Brooklyn Community Board 15, which borders Marine Park, in an email statement. “Most homeowners are fearful of a limb falling and someone being injured on their property.”

The parks department schedules regular work orders citywide for tree pruning, a type of tree maintenance that involves trimming overgrowth. These orders are carried out in each neighborhood “on a rotating basis,” the parks spokesperson said.

City residents can also alert the department about tree maintenance needs using the 311 service line, but many Marine Park residents say the parks department typically does not respond on site to service requests, and that they do not hear back after placing a complaint. Some worry their concerns are going unheard by the city.

“There’s so many dead branches on the trees,” said Marine Park resident Pauline Siringo. “I don’t know who’s looking at them, to be quite honest, when I call.”

The parks department spokesperson did not immediately respond to follow-up questions about the department’s tree maintenance protocols when a service request is filed using 311.

Trees are known to bring health and ecological benefits to cities, and are also commonly viewed as an aesthetic plus. New York City has taken to mass planting new trees in recent years to expand greenery in the city’s urban landscape.

But in New York City, trees can be a source of surprising controversy. Environmentalists urge the planting of more trees, saying they improve the local environment and promote a better quality of life. But some residents say they disrupt every-day life, like driving, walking, houses and power lines. There are competing interests to allow for tree growth to expand, and to keep tree growth in check.

In Marine Park, some residents are working to help maintain trees within the confines of what is legal. Since trees on public land are public property, residents are prohibited from chopping down or trimming trees themselves without city permission.

Some local groups, like the Marine Park Young Adults Alliance, provide regular tree care by cleaning up tree beds and making sure walkways are clear for residents, according to the group’s president, Sam Daniele.

“When you call 311 for issues, like with trees, I don't want to say they don't take it seriously,” Daniele said. “But they tend to focus on other issues first.”

For many residents, the lush greenery and quiet character of Marine Park is what brought them to the neighborhood in the first place. But a growing number of residents want those trees to be kept up with so it doesn’t disrupt daily life, and worry the city is not doing enough to help protect them from safety risks associated with tree overgrowth.

For Connie Donohue, trees bring a sense of peace to the Marine Park community, but only when well maintained. Donohue worries about the city planting new trees when she feels it is not keeping up with the trees it already has.

Tree cover “produces shade. It's good for the environment, for the oxygen. It's good for a lot of things. It's not so much that we mine the trees,” Donohue said. “If they would come up with a maintenance plan, it wouldn't be bad.”
